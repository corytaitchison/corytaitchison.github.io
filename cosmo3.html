Dark Matter

A popular candidate for dark matter is a stable, massive particle that only weakly interacts with itself and the Standard Model: known as weakly interacting massive particles (WIMPs) or cold dark matter (CDM).

  • Typically has masses on the order of GeV - TeV

On the other hand, warm dark matter is lighter but moves more rapidly (mass ~ keV); and hot dark matter is even more relativistic.

Dark matter drives the structure of the Universe because it is the dominant mass constituent; knowing how dark matter evolved helps understand the evolution of everything else.

Dark energy () does not generate structure - it drives apart matter.

CMB

The early Universe was radiation dominated, and had perturbations (caused by dark matter) with ; these therefore formed stable sound waves, which appear as the oscillations in the CMB.

The photon radiation is acting as a fluid, which feels the effect of gravity due to dark matter clumps. These fields act on the fluid and are counterracted by the internal pressure, producing sound waves.

Baryonic Acoustic Oscillation

In the early Universe, (before recombination), expansion was dominated by matter; protons, electrons and photons were in thermal equilibrium, whereas dark matter had frozen out.

This means that the effective Jeans length for baryonic matter is much greater than that for dark matter.

Therefore, density perturbation in dark matter start to grow as . On the other hand, density perturbations in baryonic matter are supported by the pressure from photons, and so only oscillate (baryonic acoustic oscillations).

Dark matter therefore had a head start in structure formation; by the time baryons decouple, there are already well-established gravitational wells, which act as seeds - expediting the formation of the baryonic structures (galaxies).

Additionally, the Silk damping effect in the photon-baryon fluid acts to smooth out any structure that develops there.

The Dark Ages

After recoupling, the Universe was transparent to radio waves and low-energy photons (less Thomson scattering); however, there now existed a fog of neutral hydrogen and helium, which visible light cannot penetrate. Moreover, there were no new sources of light (no fusion), so only photons from the decoupling process remained (and some from occasional excitation of H electrons).

However, dark matter continued to evolve, attracting the cooling gas. After million years (), large structures began to form (stars).

Metals

Anything heavier than carbon is a "metal". These metals are almost entirely formed in stars, due to stellar evolution (whereas the earlier elements were partly from Big Bang Nucleosynthesis).

At the centre of a gas cloud (star) as it is collapsing, the pressure is so enormous that we fuse H to He. At some point, the H in the centre is all converted to He - there is no radiative pressure holding up the He, and therefore they collapse under their own weight. This collapse continues until the thermal energy of the He nuclei is large enough to result in fusion of heavier elements.

The process continues, with H C,N,O,Si,Mg, etc. up to Fe.

As soon as you fuse to iron, further fusion cannot occur (binding energy per nucleon decreases) - the star can no longer hold itself up. The star therefore collapses under its own weight, into a very dense state. It then undergoes an incredible bounce, carrying off the outer (light) layers into the galaxy.

The production of metals in stars is important because metals contain many more electronic transitions, and therefore more avenues for gasses to cool. This increases the rate / efficiency of star creation.

The elemental table is an imprint of the early Universe; a snapshot in time.

Fallback

During the bounce, the innermost layers (heavier elements), however, fall back into the core. This is similar to the Stacked Ball Drop experiment.

We have observed that ancient stars are depeleted of expected heavier metals; this indicates that there was fallback onto dense nuclear seeds, possibly due to the first stars soon after reionisation turning into black holes.

Black holes are likely to be the oldest structures assembling in the early Universe, after the first stars (even older than dwarf galaxies perhaps).

The Golden Age (Evidence for the Universe Evolving)

There are several indicators which show that the Universe has continued to evolve since the first stars:

  • The star formation rate per comoving Mpc per year went through a peak ago (). This means that stars per unit volume has increased with time.
  • Similarly, the metal fraction per unit volume is also increasing (more stars means larger production).
  • The star formation rate per unit mass in a galaxy is declining with time since that peak - indicating that the efficiency of stellar production is decreasing (less favourable conditions).
  • Black hole activity (radiation observed) is also strongly correlated with the peak of star formation rate (and has been declining since).
